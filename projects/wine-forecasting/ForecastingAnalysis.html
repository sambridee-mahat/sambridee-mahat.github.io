Introduction

The Australian Wines dataset contains the monthly sales volume (in thousands of litres) for six different wine types: Fortified, Red, Rose, Sparkling, Sweet White, and Dry White. The dataset spans January 1980 to December 1994, containing 180 monthly observations. Red wine was selected for this analysis because its time series exhibits three key characteristics that make it well-suited for exponential smoothing: a clear upward trend, strong 12-month seasonality, and multiplicative seasonal behaviour where the amplitude of seasonal swings grows proportionally with the level of the series.

The objective is to forecast monthly red wine sales using a range of ETS (Error-Trend-Seasonality) smoothing models and evaluate how well each model performs against a seasonal naïve benchmark.

    hw_damped= ETS(Red ~ error("A") + trend("Ad") + season("M"))     # HW Multiplicative + Damped trend

The ETS Model Family

  1. Simple Exponential Smoothing (SES): This is the simplest model, designed for “level-only” (α) series that lack a clear trend or seasonality. It uses exponentially decaying weights, where the most recent observations have the most influence on the forecast.

  2. Holt’s Linear Trend: This model adds a trend component with parameter β, but still ignores seasonality, so it is also expected to underperform on this data.

  3. Holt-Winters (Triple Smoothing): This model incorporates all three components (level, trend, and seasonality), adding a third parameter (γ) for seasonality.

It has two main variants: i. Additive (hw_add): when seasonal swings are constant regardless of the level. ii. Multiplicative (hw_mult): Used when seasonal waves grow or shrink proportionally as the sales level rises or falls. This is the theoretically preferred specification given the growing amplitude observed in the data.

  1. Damped Trend (hw_damped): This variant adds a damping parameter (ϕ) to the Holt-Winters model, which “dampens” the trend into the future so that it eventually flattens out rather than continuing linearly forever.

SNAIVE as the Benchmark: The Seasonal Naïve (SNAIVE) model serves as the essential benchmark because it represents the simplest possible seasonal forecasting rule: assuming that the value for a given month will be exactly the same as it was in the same month of the previous year.

Across all seasonal models, the trend (β) and seasonal (γ) smoothing parameters converge to near-zero values (around 0.0001), indicating that the trend slope and seasonal pattern are extremely stable over time and update very slowly. The level parameter α is notably higher for the simpler models (SES: 0.588, Holt: 0.581) compared to the seasonal models (hw_add: 0.115, hw_mult: 0.174), reflecting that without seasonal components, these models must rely more heavily on recent observations to track the series. The damping parameter φ = 0.975 in the damped model is close to 1, suggesting the trend is only mildly dampened.

best_fit |>
  gg_tsresiduals() +
  labs(title = "Residual Diagnostics: Holt-Winters Multiplicative (AAM)")

The three-panel residual diagnostic plot examines whether the Holt-Winters Multiplicative model has successfully extracted all systematic structure from the data.

The time plot of innovation residuals shows values scattered randomly around zero throughout the 1980–1993 training period, with no obvious upward or downward drift and no persistent runs in one direction. There is some evidence of slightly increasing residual variance in the late 1980s and early 1990s, which is consistent with the multiplicative structure of the series — even after accounting for multiplicative seasonality.

The ACF of residuals shows all autocorrelation lags falling within the 95% significance bands, except for one lag at 11. This is a strong indicator that the model has successfully removed both the trend and the 12-month seasonal autocorrelation structure from the data.

The histogram of residuals is approximately bell-shaped and centred close to zero. The distribution has slightly heavier tails than a perfect normal, which may be partly attributable to the few extreme spike months noted in the time plot.

The Ljung-Box p-value is used to determine whether data is randomly distributed or exhibit some significant autocorrelation. Ho: residuals are white noise Ha: autocorrelation is present

For, hw_mult, the p-value is 0.6, which is greater than 0.05. Therefore, we don’t have enough evidence to reject the null hypothesis. It implies the residuals are random and it has extracted everything statistically extractable from the data.

The cross-model Ljung-Box comparison is instructive. All three ETS seasonal models (hw_add: p = 0.185, hw_damped: p = 0.663, hw_mult: p = 0.600) pass the white noise test comfortably. The seasonal naïve model, by contrast, fails the test with a p-value of 0.000234, indicating its residuals retain significant autocorrelation.

    ifelse(abs(me_val) < 100,

# Step 4: Final recommendation
if (mase_val < 1 & lb_result$lb_pvalue > 0.05) {
  cat("RECOMMENDATION: Model is adequate for deployment.\n")
  cat("Monitor forecast errors monthly and refit quarterly.\n")
} else if (mase_val < 1 & lb_result$lb_pvalue <= 0.05) {
  cat("RECOMMENDATION: Model beats benchmark but residuals have structure.\n")
